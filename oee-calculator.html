What is OEE and Why Does It Matter?

Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) is the gold standard metric for measuring manufacturing productivity. Developed from the principles of Total Productive Maintenance (TPM), OEE quantifies how effectively a manufacturing operation uses its equipment compared to its full potential — combining three key factors into a single, actionable percentage.

The OEE Formula

OEE is calculated by multiplying three components, each expressed as a percentage:

  • Availability — The proportion of scheduled time that the operation is available to run. It accounts for downtime caused by equipment breakdowns, changeovers, and setup time.
  • Performance — How fast the process runs compared to its designed ideal speed. It captures speed losses from micro-stops and slow cycles.
  • Quality — The ratio of good parts produced to total parts started. It reflects losses from defects, rework, and startup rejects.

So if a machine runs 90% of the time it was scheduled (Availability), operates at 90% of its ideal speed (Performance), and produces 90% good parts (Quality), the OEE score is 0.90 × 0.90 × 0.90 = 72.9% — well below what it may appear at first glance.

40% – 64%Below AverageCommon in plants without formal TPM programs. Major losses in at least one category.
< 40%LowUnacceptable for most industries. Likely experiencing major unplanned downtime or high defect rates.

How to Use OEE to Improve Operations

The power of OEE lies not just in the final number, but in identifying which of the three components is dragging performance down. This is because each component maps directly to the Six Big Losses defined in TPM:

  • Availability losses: Unplanned downtime (breakdowns), planned downtime (changeovers)
  • Performance losses: Minor stops (jams, sensor faults), reduced speed
  • Quality losses: Production rejects, startup rejects

For example, if your Availability is 95% but Performance is only 60%, the problem is not breakdowns — it's speed. The solution is not more maintenance, but cycle time analysis and operator training.

OEE for Different Industries

World-class OEE targets vary by industry. Discrete manufacturing (automotive, electronics) typically targets 85%+. Process industries (food, chemical, pharmaceutical) often run lower due to mandatory cleaning cycles. Semiconductor fabs may track OEE at 90%+ due to the extreme cost of idle equipment.
